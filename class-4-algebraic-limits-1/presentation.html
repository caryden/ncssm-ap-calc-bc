Remember This?

\[\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} \approx 6\]

The Limit Laws

Rules that let us break complex limits into simple pieces

Starting Simple

Constant Law \(\lim_{x \to a} c = c\)
Identity Law \(\lim_{x \to a} x = a\)

The limit of a constant is that constant. The limit of x is the target value.

Combining Limits

If \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\) and \(\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M\), then:

Sum Law \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) + g(x)] = L + M\)
Difference Law \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - g(x)] = L - M\)
Constant Multiple \(\lim_{x \to a} [c \cdot f(x)] = c \cdot L\)

Products and Quotients

Product Law \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) \cdot g(x)] = L \cdot M\)
Quotient Law \(\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}\) (if M ≠ 0)

The quotient law requires the denominator's limit to be nonzero!

Powers and Roots

Power Law \(\lim_{x \to a} [f(x)]^n = L^n\)
Root Law \(\lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} = \sqrt[n]{L}\) (with appropriate conditions)

Limit Laws Reference

Constant
\(\lim c = c\)
Identity
\(\lim x = a\)
Sum
\(\lim[f + g] = L + M\)
Difference
\(\lim[f - g] = L - M\)
Constant Multiple
\(\lim[cf] = cL\)
Product
\(\lim[f \cdot g] = L \cdot M\)
Quotient
\(\lim\frac{f}{g} = \frac{L}{M}\) (M ≠ 0)
Power
\(\lim[f^n] = L^n\)

These rules are your algebraic toolkit for limits

For polynomials: Just plug in!

\[\lim_{x \to a} p(x) = p(a)\]

Why Direct Substitution Works

Polynomials are built from operations that preserve limits:

1
\(\lim_{x \to a} x = a\)
Identity Law
2
\(\lim_{x \to a} x^2 = a \cdot a = a^2\)
Product Law
3
\(\lim_{x \to a} cx^n = c \cdot a^n\)
Constant Multiple + Power
4
Add terms together...
Sum Law

Example: Polynomial Limit

\[\lim_{x \to 2} (3x^2 + 5x - 1)\]
1
Substitute x = 2:
2
\(3(2)^2 + 5(2) - 1\)
3
\(3(4) + 10 - 1 = 12 + 10 - 1\)
✓
= 21

Example: Higher Degree

\[\lim_{x \to -1} (x^4 - 2x^2 + 7)\]
1
Substitute x = -1:
2
\((-1)^4 - 2(-1)^2 + 7\)
3
\(1 - 2(1) + 7 = 1 - 2 + 7\)
✓
= 6

Rational Functions

Direct substitution works... when the denominator isn't zero

\[\lim_{x \to a} \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \frac{p(a)}{q(a)} \quad \text{if } q(a) \neq 0\]

Example: Rational Function

\[\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x + 1}{x - 1}\]
1
Check denominator at x = 2:
2 - 1 = 1 ≠ 0 ✓
2
Direct substitution:
\(\frac{2 + 1}{2 - 1} = \frac{3}{1}\)
✓
= 3

Example: More Complex

\[\lim_{x \to 4} \frac{x^2 - 1}{x + 2}\]
1
Check denominator at x = 4:
4 + 2 = 6 ≠ 0 ✓
2
Direct substitution:
\(\frac{4^2 - 1}{4 + 2} = \frac{16 - 1}{6} = \frac{15}{6}\)
✓
= 5/2

But What About This?

\[\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\]
1
Check denominator at x = 2:
2 - 2 = 0 ✗
2
Check numerator at x = 2:
4 - 4 = 0
0/0

Direct substitution fails!

\(\frac{0}{0}\)

is indeterminate

It doesn't mean 0, 1, or undefined. It means we need more work.

The 0/0 Form Means...

  • Something interesting is happening
  • We need to simplify first
  • The limit may still exist!
  • Direct substitution alone won't work

Key Takeaways

  • Limit laws let us break complex limits into pieces
  • For polynomials: direct substitution always works
  • For rational functions: check the denominator first
  • 0/0 is indeterminate—not the final answer
