Integration by Parts

Class 36 | CED Topic 6.11

The Product Rule in Reverse

Warm-Up

Find the derivative of \( x \cdot e^x \)

\( \frac{d}{dx}[x \cdot e^x] = x \cdot e^x + e^x \cdot 1 = e^x(x + 1) \)

The product rule creates a sum. Can we reverse this process?

The Challenge

How do we evaluate \( \displaystyle\int x \cdot e^x \, dx \)?

This is a product of two functions.
U-substitution doesn't work directly.

We need a new technique!

Deriving the Formula

Start with the Product Rule:

\( \frac{d}{dx}[u \cdot v] = u \cdot \frac{dv}{dx} + v \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \)

Integrate both sides:

\( u \cdot v = \int u \, dv + \int v \, du \)

Rearrange:

\( \int u \, dv = u \cdot v - \int v \, du \)

Integration by Parts Formula

\( \displaystyle\int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du \)

The goal: Transform a difficult integral into an easier one

Choosing u: The LIATE Rule

Choose \(u\) to be the function that comes first in this list:

L I A T E
L
Logarithmic
I
Inverse trig
A
Algebraic
T
Trigonometric
E
Exponential

This is a guideline, not a strict rule. Choose \(u\) so that \(du\) simplifies!

Example 1: \( \displaystyle\int x e^x \, dx \)

Setup:

Let \( u = x \) → \( du = dx \)
Let \( dv = e^x \, dx \) → \( v = e^x \)

Apply formula:

\( \int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du \)

\( \int x e^x \, dx = x \cdot e^x - \int e^x \, dx = xe^x - e^x + C \)

✓ Check: \( \frac{d}{dx}[xe^x - e^x] = e^x + xe^x - e^x = xe^x \) ✓

Example 2: \( \displaystyle\int x \sin x \, dx \)

Setup (LIATE: A before T):

\( u = x \), \( du = dx \)
\( dv = \sin x \, dx \), \( v = -\cos x \)

\( \int x \sin x \, dx = -x\cos x - \int (-\cos x) \, dx \)
\( = -x\cos x + \int \cos x \, dx = -x\cos x + \sin x + C \)

Example 3: \( \displaystyle\int \ln x \, dx \)

Key insight: Write this as \( \int 1 \cdot \ln x \, dx \)

\( u = \ln x \), \( du = \frac{1}{x} dx \)
\( dv = 1 \cdot dx \), \( v = x \)

\( \int \ln x \, dx = x \ln x - \int x \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dx \)
\( = x \ln x - \int 1 \, dx = x \ln x - x + C \)

Repeated Integration by Parts

Sometimes one application isn't enough!

\( \displaystyle\int x^2 e^x \, dx \)

After one IBP, you'll have \( \int x e^x \, dx \)
which requires IBP again!

Tabular Method

For repeated IBP with polynomials, use the tabular method:

\( \displaystyle\int x^2 e^x \, dx \)

Sign D (differentiate) I (integrate)
+ \( x^2 \) \( e^x \)
− \( 2x \) \( e^x \)
+ \( 2 \) \( e^x \)
− \( 0 \) \( e^x \)

Reading the Tabular Method

Multiply diagonally with alternating signs:

\( \int x^2 e^x \, dx = (+)(x^2)(e^x) + (-)(2x)(e^x) + (+)(2)(e^x) + C \)
\( = e^x(x^2 - 2x + 2) + C \)

Special Case: Cycling

\( \displaystyle\int e^x \sin x \, dx \)

First IBP: Let \( u = e^x \), \( dv = \sin x \, dx \)

\( = -e^x \cos x + \int e^x \cos x \, dx \)

Second IBP: Let \( u = e^x \), \( dv = \cos x \, dx \)

\( = -e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x - \int e^x \sin x \, dx \)

The original integral appears on the right! Solve algebraically.

Solving the Cycling Case

Let \( I = \int e^x \sin x \, dx \)

We found: \( I = -e^x \cos x + e^x \sin x - I \)

\( 2I = e^x(\sin x - \cos x) \)
\( I = \frac{e^x(\sin x - \cos x)}{2} + C \)

IBP with Definite Integrals

\( \displaystyle\int_a^b u \, dv = \left[uv\right]_a^b - \int_a^b v \, du \)

Apply the limits to the \(uv\) term, then evaluate the remaining integral.

Key Takeaways

• IBP reverses the product rule: \( \int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du \)

• LIATE helps choose \(u\): Logs, Inverse trig, Algebraic, Trig, Exponential

• Sometimes you need repeated applications

• Tabular method speeds up polynomial cases

• Cycling cases require algebraic solving
