Unit 2: Differentiation Fundamentals

The Product Rule

Differentiating Products of Functions

Class 13 | CED 2.7 | NCSSM

What About Products?

We know: \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[f + g] = f' + g'\)

So maybe...

\(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[f \cdot g] \stackrel{?}{=} f' \cdot g'\)

ü§î

Let's Test It!

Let \(f(x) = x\) and \(g(x) = x\)

Actual:

\(\frac{d}{dx}[x \cdot x] = \frac{d}{dx}[x^2] = 2x\)

Naive Guess:

\(f' \cdot g' = 1 \cdot 1 = 1\)

\(2x \neq 1\), so our guess is WRONG!

The derivative of a product is NOT the product of derivatives!

We need a new rule...

The Product Rule

\(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \cdot g(x)] = f'(x) \cdot g(x) + f(x) \cdot g'(x)\)

"\(f\) prime \(g\) + \(f\) \(g\) prime"

How to Remember

Leave first \(f\)
√ó
d-second \(g'\)
+
Leave second \(g\)
√ó
d-first \(f'\)

"First d-second plus second d-first"

Why Does It Work?

Both dimensions contribute to the rate of area change

Testing with \(f(x) = x\), \(g(x) = x\)

\(f = x \Rightarrow f' = 1\)

\(g = x \Rightarrow g' = 1\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[x \cdot x] = (1)(x) + (x)(1)\)

\(= x + x = 2x\)

‚úì

It works!

Example 1

\(h(x) = (3x^2)(x^3 + 1)\)

\(f = 3x^2 \Rightarrow f' = 6x\)

\(g = x^3 + 1 \Rightarrow g' = 3x^2\)

\(h' = (6x)(x^3 + 1) + (3x^2)(3x^2)\)

\(= 6x^4 + 6x + 9x^4\)

\(h'(x) = 15x^4 + 6x\)

Check: Expand First

\(h(x) = (3x^2)(x^3 + 1) = 3x^5 + 3x^2\)

\(h'(x) = 15x^4 + 6x\) ‚úì

Same answer! (But expansion isn't always possible)

Example 2

\(h(x) = (x^2 + 1)\sqrt{x}\)

\(f = x^2 + 1 \Rightarrow f' = 2x\)

\(g = x^{1/2} \Rightarrow g' = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}\)

\(h' = (2x)(\sqrt{x}) + (x^2 + 1)\left(\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\right)\)

\(h'(x) = 2x\sqrt{x} + \frac{x^2 + 1}{2\sqrt{x}}\)

Example 3 (Preview)

\(h(x) = x^2 e^x\)

Note: \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\) (we'll prove this soon!)

\(h' = (2x)(e^x) + (x^2)(e^x)\)

\(h'(x) = e^x(2x + x^2) = e^x(x^2 + 2x)\)

When to Use Product Rule?

Use Product Rule

  • ‚Ä¢ Transcendental functions (\(e^x\), \(\sin x\), \(\ln x\))
  • ‚Ä¢ Complex factors
  • ‚Ä¢ Can't easily expand

Just Expand

  • ‚Ä¢ Simple polynomials
  • ‚Ä¢ Quick expansion
  • ‚Ä¢ Easier calculation

Visualizing \(f \cdot g\) and \((f \cdot g)'\)

COMMON MISTAKE

\(\frac{d}{dx}[f \cdot g] = f' \cdot g'\)

\(\frac{d}{dx}[f \cdot g] = f' g + f g'\)

Key Takeaways

  • \(\frac{d}{dx}[fg] \neq f'g'\) ‚Äî This is WRONG!
  • Product rule: \((fg)' = f'g + fg'\)
  • Remember: "First d-second + second d-first"
  • Choose wisely: sometimes expansion is easier
