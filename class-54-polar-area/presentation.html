Area in Polar Coordinates

Class 54 | CED Topics 9.8, 9.9

Finding Areas Enclosed by Polar Curves

Warm-Up

What is the area of a circular sector with radius \( r \) and angle \( \theta \)?

\( A = \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta \)

This is the key to polar area!

Finding Polar Area

Divide the region into thin "pie slices" (sectors)

Each slice has: • Radius ≈ \( r = f(\theta) \)
• Angle = \( d\theta \)

Area of one slice: \( dA = \frac{1}{2}[f(\theta)]^2 \, d\theta \)

Polar Area Formula

\( A = \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \frac{1}{2}[f(\theta)]^2 \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} r^2 \, d\theta \)

Don't forget the \( \frac{1}{2} \) factor!

Polar Area: Sector Approximation

Example 1: Circle

Find the area enclosed by \( r = 2 \).

Full circle: \( \theta \) from \( 0 \) to \( 2\pi \)

\( A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} (2)^2 \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 4 \cdot 2\pi = 4\pi \)

✓ Matches \( \pi r^2 = \pi(2)^2 = 4\pi \) ✓

Example 2: Cardioid

Find the area enclosed by \( r = 1 + \cos\theta \).

Full cardioid: \( \theta \) from \( 0 \) to \( 2\pi \)

\( A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} (1 + \cos\theta)^2 \, d\theta \)

Expand: \( (1 + \cos\theta)^2 = 1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta \)

Cardioid Area (continued)

Use \( \cos^2\theta = \frac{1 + \cos 2\theta}{2} \)

\( A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} \left(1 + 2\cos\theta + \frac{1+\cos 2\theta}{2}\right) d\theta \)
\( = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{3\theta}{2} + 2\sin\theta + \frac{\sin 2\theta}{4}\right]_0^{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{3 \cdot 2\pi}{2} = \frac{3\pi}{2} \)

Area of Rose Curves

For \( r = a\cos(n\theta) \) or \( r = a\sin(n\theta) \):

Key insight: Use symmetry!

Find the area of ONE petal, then multiply.

Number of petals: \( n \) (if \( n \) is odd) or \( 2n \) (if \( n \) is even)

Example 3: Rose Curve

Find the total area enclosed by \( r = 2\sin(3\theta) \).

3 petals, one petal from \( \theta = 0 \) to \( \theta = \frac{\pi}{3} \)

\( A_{\text{petal}} = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/3} [2\sin(3\theta)]^2 \, d\theta = 2\int_0^{\pi/3} \sin^2(3\theta) \, d\theta \)

Using \( \sin^2 u = \frac{1-\cos 2u}{2} \): \( A_{\text{petal}} = \frac{\pi}{3} \)

Total: \( 3 \cdot \frac{\pi}{3} = \pi \)

Area Between Polar Curves

\( A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \left([r_{\text{outer}}]^2 - [r_{\text{inner}}]^2\right) d\theta \)

Similar to area between Cartesian curves!

Finding Intersection Points

To find where \( r = f(\theta) \) and \( r = g(\theta) \) intersect:

1. Set \( f(\theta) = g(\theta) \) and solve for \( \theta \)

2. Check if origin is included (when either \( r = 0 \))

Warning: Curves may pass through the same point at different \( \theta \) values!

Example 4: Area Between Curves

Find the area inside \( r = 2 \) and outside \( r = 2(1 - \cos\theta) \).

Intersection: \( 2 = 2(1 - \cos\theta) \) → \( \cos\theta = 0 \) → \( \theta = \pm\frac{\pi}{2} \)

\( A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} \left[(2)^2 - [2(1-\cos\theta)]^2\right] d\theta \)

Key Takeaways

• Polar area: \( A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2 \, d\theta \)

• Based on sector area: \( \frac{1}{2}r^2\theta \)

• Use symmetry for rose curves and cardioids

• Between curves: \( \frac{1}{2}\int (r_{outer}^2 - r_{inner}^2) \, d\theta \)

• Find intersections by setting \( r_1 = r_2 \)
