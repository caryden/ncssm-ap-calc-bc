U-Substitution

Reversing the Chain Rule

Class 35 | CED Topic 6.9 | AP Calculus BC | NCSSM

How do we integrate \( \int 2x \cos(x^2) \, dx \)?

The Chain Rule in Reverse

Chain Rule

\( \frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \)

Reversing It

\( \int f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \, dx = f(g(x)) + C \)

U-Substitution Method

Steps

1 Identify the "inside function" and let \( u = \) that function
2 Find \( \frac{du}{dx} \), then solve for \( du \)
3 Rewrite the integral entirely in terms of \( u \)
4 Integrate with respect to \( u \)
5 Substitute back to get the answer in terms of \( x \)

Example: \( \int 2x \cos(x^2) \, dx \)

1 Let \( u = x^2 \) (the inside function)
2 \( \frac{du}{dx} = 2x \), so \( du = 2x \, dx \)
3 \( \int 2x \cos(x^2) \, dx = \int \cos(u) \, du \)
4 \( = \sin(u) + C \)
5 \( = \sin(x^2) + C \)

Example: \( \int (3x+1)^5 \, dx \)

1 Let \( u = 3x + 1 \)
2 \( du = 3 \, dx \), so \( dx = \frac{1}{3} du \)
3 \( \int (3x+1)^5 \, dx = \int u^5 \cdot \frac{1}{3} \, du = \frac{1}{3} \int u^5 \, du \)
4 \( = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{u^6}{6} + C = \frac{u^6}{18} + C \)
5 \( = \frac{(3x+1)^6}{18} + C \)

Example: \( \int x e^{x^2} \, dx \)

1 Let \( u = x^2 \), so \( du = 2x \, dx \)
2 Need \( x \, dx \), have \( 2x \, dx = du \), so \( x \, dx = \frac{1}{2} du \)
3 \( \int x e^{x^2} \, dx = \int e^u \cdot \frac{1}{2} \, du = \frac{1}{2} \int e^u \, du \)
4 \( = \frac{1}{2} e^u + C = \frac{1}{2} e^{x^2} + C \)

Recognizing U-Sub Patterns

Inside Ã— Its Derivative

\( \int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \, dx \)

Composite Functions

\( \int (ax+b)^n \, dx \)

Trig of Linear

\( \int \sin(ax) \, dx \)

Natural Log

\( \int \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \, dx = \ln|f(x)| + C \)

The Natural Log Pattern

Key Pattern

\( \int \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \, dx = \ln|f(x)| + C \)

Ex \( \int \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1} \, dx = \ln|x^2 + 1| + C = \ln(x^2 + 1) + C \)
Ex \( \int \tan(x) \, dx = \int \frac{\sin x}{\cos x} \, dx = -\ln|\cos x| + C \)

U-Sub with Definite Integrals

Two Approaches

Method 1: Find indefinite integral, then evaluate at original limits

Method 2: Change the limits when you substitute!

If \( u = g(x) \), then when \( x = a \), \( u = g(a) \)

and when \( x = b \), \( u = g(b) \)

Example: \( \int_0^2 x(x^2+1)^3 \, dx \)

1 Let \( u = x^2 + 1 \), \( du = 2x \, dx \), so \( x \, dx = \frac{1}{2} du \)
2 Change limits: When \( x = 0 \), \( u = 1 \). When \( x = 2 \), \( u = 5 \)
3 \( = \frac{1}{2} \int_1^5 u^3 \, du = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{u^4}{4} \right]_1^5 \)
4 \( = \frac{1}{8} (5^4 - 1^4) = \frac{1}{8}(625 - 1) = \frac{624}{8} = 78 \)

U-Substitution Summary

Identify: Look for "inside function" and its derivative

Substitute: Let \( u \) = inside, find \( du \), rewrite integral

Integrate: Solve in terms of \( u \)

Back-substitute: Replace \( u \) with original expression

Definite integrals: Change limits OR substitute back before evaluating

Exit Ticket

2. Evaluate \( \int_0^1 \frac{x}{(x^2+1)^2} \, dx \)
