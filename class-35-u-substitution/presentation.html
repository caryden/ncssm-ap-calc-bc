Unit 6: Integration and Accumulation of Change

U-Substitution

The Chain Rule in Reverse

Class 35 | CED 6.9 | NCSSM

This Doesn't Match Our Rules

\(\displaystyle \int 2x \cos(x^2)\,dx\)

• Not just \(\cos(x)\)

• Not a simple product we can separate

• What should we do?

Wait - This Looks Familiar!

If we differentiated \(\sin(x^2)\)...

\(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x^2)] = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x\)

That's exactly our integrand!

✓ \(\displaystyle \int 2x \cos(x^2)\,dx = \sin(x^2) + C\)

U-substitution is the chain rule running backwards

"We're recognizing that a complex-looking integral has a composition structure that the chain rule would create."

From Chain Rule to Substitution

Chain rule:

\(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[F(g(x))] = F'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) = f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\)

Reversing:

\(\displaystyle \int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\,dx = F(g(x)) + C\)

If the derivative of the inner function appears as a factor, we can integrate!

The Substitution

Let \(u = g(x)\) (the inner function)

Then \(du = g'(x)\,dx\)

Transform:

\(\displaystyle \int f(\underbrace{g(x)}_{u}) \cdot \underbrace{g'(x)\,dx}_{du} \quad\to\quad \int f(u)\,du\)

The complex integral becomes a simple one!

Step-by-Step Procedure

  • 1. Identify \(u\) (the "inside" function)
  • 2. Compute \(du = u'(x)\,dx\)
  • 3. Solve for \(dx\) or a product in terms of \(du\)
  • 4. Substitute: replace ALL x-expressions with u
  • 5. Integrate in terms of \(u\)
  • 6. Back-substitute: replace \(u\) with \(g(x)\)
  • 7. Check by differentiating

How to Choose u

  • Look for a function whose derivative ALSO appears
  • Choose the "inside" of a composition
  • Choose the more complicated factor
  • Goal: make the integral SIMPLER after substitution

These are guidelines, not strict rules

Example 1: Basic U-Substitution

\(\displaystyle \int (2x+1)^5\,dx\)

Let \(u = 2x + 1\)

\(du = 2\,dx\), so \(dx = \frac{du}{2}\)

\(\displaystyle \int u^5 \cdot \frac{du}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{u^6}{6} + C\)

\(\displaystyle = \frac{(2x+1)^6}{12} + C\)

When du Doesn't Match Exactly

\(\displaystyle \int x\sqrt{x^2+4}\,dx\)

If \(u = x^2 + 4\), then \(du = 2x\,dx\)

But we only have \(x\,dx\), not \(2x\,dx\)

Solution: \(x\,dx = \frac{1}{2}du\)

Factor out the constant adjustment

Example 2: Constant Adjustment

\(\displaystyle \int x\sqrt{x^2+4}\,dx\)

\(u = x^2 + 4\)

\(du = 2x\,dx\) \(\Rightarrow x\,dx = \frac{du}{2}\)

\(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{u} \cdot \frac{du}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2} + C\)

\(\displaystyle = \frac{1}{3}(x^2+4)^{3/2} + C\)

Example 3: Exponential Composition

\(\displaystyle \int e^{\sin x} \cos x\,dx\)

\(u = \sin x\)

\(du = \cos x\,dx\) (appears exactly!)

\(\displaystyle \int e^u\,du = e^u + C\)

\(\displaystyle = e^{\sin x} + C\)

When du appears exactly, substitution is clean

Example 4: The 1/u Pattern

\(\displaystyle \int \tan x\,dx = \int \frac{\sin x}{\cos x}\,dx\)

\(u = \cos x\)

\(du = -\sin x\,dx\) \(\Rightarrow \sin x\,dx = -du\)

\(\displaystyle \int \frac{-du}{u} = -\ln|u| + C\)

\(\displaystyle = -\ln|\cos x| + C = \ln|\sec x| + C\)

Visualizing the Substitution

The complex integral transforms into a simple one

Definite Integrals with U-Sub

Method 1: Change Bounds

  • When \(x = a\), \(u = g(a)\)
  • When \(x = b\), \(u = g(b)\)
  • Integrate entirely in \(u\)
  • No back-substitution needed

Method 2: Back-Substitute First

  • Find indefinite integral in \(x\)
  • Then evaluate at original bounds
  • Same result, different path

Example 5: Changing Bounds

\(\displaystyle \int_0^1 2xe^{x^2}\,dx\)

\(u = x^2\), \(du = 2x\,dx\)

When \(x = 0\): \(u = 0\)

When \(x = 1\): \(u = 1\)

\(\displaystyle \int_0^1 e^u\,du = e^u\Big|_0^1\)

\(\displaystyle = e - 1\)

Example 6: Back-Substitution Method

\(\displaystyle \int_0^2 x(x^2+1)^3\,dx\)

\(u = x^2 + 1\), \(du = 2x\,dx\), \(x\,dx = \frac{du}{2}\)

Indefinite: \(\frac{1}{2}\int u^3\,du = \frac{u^4}{8} + C = \frac{(x^2+1)^4}{8} + C\)

Evaluate: \(\frac{(5)^4}{8} - \frac{(1)^4}{8} = \frac{625 - 1}{8}\)

\(\displaystyle = 78\)

Patterns to Recognize

Pattern u = Result
\(\int [f(x)]^n f'(x)\,dx\) \(f(x)\) \(\frac{[f(x)]^{n+1}}{n+1} + C\)
\(\int e^{f(x)} f'(x)\,dx\) \(f(x)\) \(e^{f(x)} + C\)
\(\int \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}\,dx\) \(f(x)\) \(\ln|f(x)| + C\)

Trigonometric Patterns

Pattern u = Result
\(\int \sin(f(x)) f'(x)\,dx\) \(f(x)\) \(-\cos(f(x)) + C\)
\(\int \cos(f(x)) f'(x)\,dx\) \(f(x)\) \(\sin(f(x)) + C\)

Key: The derivative of the inner function appears as a factor

Not every integral can be solved by substitution

If the derivative of your chosen \(u\) doesn't appear (even with constants), try a different approach

Avoid These Errors

  • ✗ DON'T forget to convert dx to du
  • ✗ DON'T leave u in your final answer
  • ✗ DON'T forget the constant adjustment
  • ✗ DON'T mix x's and u's in the same integral
  • ✓ DO verify by differentiating

Practice 1

\(\displaystyle \int x^2(x^3-1)^4\,dx\)

Hint: What should u be?

Time: 2 minutes

Solution

\(u = x^3 - 1\), \(du = 3x^2\,dx\), \(x^2\,dx = \frac{du}{3}\)

\(\displaystyle \int u^4 \cdot \frac{du}{3} = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{u^5}{5} + C\)

\(\displaystyle = \frac{(x^3-1)^5}{15} + C\)

Practice 2: Definite Integral

\(\displaystyle \int_0^{\pi/2} \cos x \cdot e^{\sin x}\,dx\)

Time: 3 minutes

Solution

\(u = \sin x\), \(du = \cos x\,dx\)

Bounds: \(x=0 \Rightarrow u=0\); \(x=\pi/2 \Rightarrow u=1\)

\(\displaystyle \int_0^1 e^u\,du = e^u\Big|_0^1\)

\(\displaystyle = e - 1\)

Key Takeaways

  • U-substitution reverses the chain rule
  • Choose u so that du (or a constant multiple) appears
  • ALL x's must become u's before integrating
  • ALWAYS back-substitute (replace u with original)
  • For definite integrals: change bounds OR back-substitute
  • Verify by differentiating

Exit Ticket

Evaluate:

\(\displaystyle \int_1^2 \frac{x}{x^2+1}\,dx\)

Show all steps including choice of u, du, and substitution
