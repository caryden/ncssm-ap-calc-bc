# AP Calculus BC Course Plan

## Document Purpose

This document defines the **pedagogical philosophy** and **instructional approach** for the AP Calculus BC course at NCSSM. It serves as the foundation for all downstream artifacts: lesson plans, presentation specs, and presentations.

All course materials should be consistent with this plan. When making decisions about content, visualization, or assessment, refer back to this document.

---

## Course Identity

**Course:** AP Calculus BC
**Institution:** North Carolina School of Science and Mathematics (NCSSM)
**Delivery:** Simultaneous in-classroom (Durham) and Zoom (Morganton)
**Duration:** 64 class sessions, 50 minutes each
**Students:** High school juniors and seniors with strong math backgrounds

## Pedagogical Philosophy

### Core Belief

> **Calculus is a framework for understanding change and accumulation, not a collection of procedures to memorize.**

Students who complete this course should:
1. Understand **why** calculus works, not just how to apply it
2. See calculus as a unified subject, not disconnected techniques
3. Feel capable of reasoning about novel problems
4. Appreciate calculus as a human achievement with rich history

### Guiding Principles

#### 1. Understanding Over Memorization

We prioritize conceptual understanding over procedural fluency. Students should be able to explain **why** a technique works, not just apply it.

**In practice:**
- Lead with intuition before formulas
- Ask "why does this make sense?" frequently
- Connect procedures to underlying concepts
- Avoid presenting formulas without derivation

#### 2. Visual-First Approach

Most calculus concepts have natural visual interpretations. We lead with these visuals to build intuition before introducing symbolic representations.

**Based on:** 3Blue1Brown's "Essence of Calculus" philosophy
> "Come away feeling like you could have invented calculus."

**In practice:**
- Every major concept has a D3/Desmos/GeoGebra visualization
- Animations show mathematical relationships dynamically
- Static diagrams are used only when animation adds nothing
- Visual representations appear before algebraic ones

#### 3. Rule of Four (G.N.A.W.)

Every concept is presented in four complementary ways:

| Representation | Abbreviation | Strength |
|----------------|--------------|----------|
| **G**raphical | G | Visual intuition, qualitative understanding |
| **N**umerical | N | Concrete values, computational verification |
| **A**nalytical | A | Precision, rigor, proof |
| **W**ords (Verbal) | W | Communication, interpretation |

**In practice:**
- Each major concept has slides for all four representations
- Students practice translating between representations
- Assessments include multi-representation problems
- The mnemonic "G.N.A.W." is used explicitly with students

#### 4. Address Misconceptions Directly

Research identifies common student errors in calculus. We explicitly address these rather than hoping students avoid them.

**In practice:**
- Each lesson plan identifies relevant misconceptions
- Presentations include "common errors" slides
- Visualizations are designed to contradict misconceptions
- Assessment items target known error patterns

#### 5. Connection Over Isolation

Calculus topics are deeply interconnected. We emphasize these connections to build a unified understanding.

**Key connections to highlight:**
- Derivative and integral as inverse operations (FTC)
- Limit as the foundation of derivatives, integrals, and series
- Rate of change ↔ accumulation duality
- Approximation techniques across topics (linearization, Riemann sums, Taylor polynomials)

#### 6. Active Learning

Students learn by doing, not just watching. Every class includes opportunities for active engagement.

**In practice:**
- 15-20 minutes per class for practice/exploration
- "Think-pair-share" moments built into presentations
- Interactive visualizations for student exploration
- Regular low-stakes formative assessment

## Instructional Design

### Backward Design (Understanding by Design)

All course materials are developed using backward design:

1. **Stage 1: Desired Results**
   - What should students understand and be able to do?
   - Defined by AP CED learning objectives

2. **Stage 2: Assessment Evidence**
   - How will we know students have achieved objectives?
   - Designed before instruction

3. **Stage 3: Learning Plan**
   - What experiences will support learning?
   - Developed to meet objectives, not cover content

### Class Session Structure

Each 50-minute session follows this structure:

| Phase | Duration | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Opening** | 5 min | Connect to prior learning, preview objectives |
| **Instruction** | 20-25 min | Develop concepts using Rule of Four |
| **Practice** | 15-20 min | Active learning, exploration, problem-solving |
| **Closing** | 5 min | Summarize, preview next class, exit ticket |

### Technology Integration

Technology serves pedagogy, not the reverse. We use:

| Tool | Purpose | When |
|------|---------|------|
| **D3.js** | Custom interactive visualizations | Animated mathematical relationships |
| **Desmos** | Function exploration | Student-driven parameter exploration |
| **GeoGebra** | 3D visualization | Volumes, parametric curves |
| **TI Calculator** | Computation | Practice for AP exam calculator sections |

**Principle:** Use the right tool for the pedagogical goal. Don't use technology just because it's available.

## Rule of Four Implementation

### Balance Guidelines

Not every concept needs equal emphasis on all four representations. The balance depends on the topic:

| Topic Type | Emphasis | Example |
|------------|----------|---------|
| Foundational concepts | G > A > W > N | Derivative definition |
| Computational techniques | A > N > G > W | Chain rule |
| Applied problems | W > G > A > N | Related rates |
| Approximation methods | N > G > A > W | Riemann sums |

### Representation Standards

#### Graphical (G)
- Clear axis labels with appropriate scale
- Function curves in blue, derivatives in orange, integrals in green
- Animated transitions showing mathematical relationships
- Interactive elements where they add value

#### Numerical (N)
- Tables with clear headers
- Appropriate significant figures
- Show pattern before generalization
- Calculator use for complex computations

#### Analytical (A)
- Proper mathematical notation (MathJax)
- Step-by-step derivations with justification
- Clear distinction between equality and approximation
- Limit notation maintained until evaluated

#### Verbal (W)
- Precise mathematical vocabulary
- "In words" descriptions of procedures
- Interpretation of results in context
- Real-world connections where appropriate

## Misconception Awareness

### Priority Misconceptions by Unit

Based on calculus education research, these misconceptions require explicit attention:

#### Unit 1: Limits
- Limit = function value (not always)
- "Approaches" means "reaches" (no, it's about nearby behavior)
- ∞ is a number you can compute with (it's a concept)

#### Unit 2-3: Derivatives
- Forgetting inner derivative in chain rule
- Confusing f and f' graphs
- "Differentiable implies continuous" reversal (wrong direction)

#### Unit 6: Integration
- Integral = antiderivative only (also area/accumulation)
- Forgetting +C in indefinite integrals
- Expecting product/quotient rules for integrals

#### Unit 10: Series
- "Convergent" means "equals the limit" (no, it's about behavior)
- Ratio test gives exact value (no, just convergence)
- All series either converge or diverge to ∞ (conditional convergence)

### Addressing Strategy

For each misconception:
1. **Identify** in lesson plan
2. **Visualize** with counterexample
3. **Explicitly discuss** in presentation
4. **Assess** with targeted questions

## Assessment Philosophy

### Purpose of Assessment

Assessment serves three purposes:
1. **Learning:** Assessment as a learning activity (formative)
2. **Feedback:** Information for student and teacher
3. **Certification:** Evidence of mastery (summative)

### Assessment Types

| Type | Frequency | Purpose | Format |
|------|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Exit Tickets** | Every class | Quick comprehension check | 1-2 questions |
| **Progress Checks** | After each topic cluster | Identify gaps | AP Classroom |
| **Quizzes** | Weekly | Skill reinforcement | Mixed format |
| **Unit Tests** | After each unit | Summative evaluation | AP-style MC + FRQ |
| **Practice Exams** | 2 before AP exam | Full simulation | Complete AP format |

### AP Exam Alignment

All assessments mirror AP exam format:
- Multiple choice with 4 options
- Free response with clear rubric
- Calculator and non-calculator sections
- Justification requirements explicit

### Rubric Language

Use AP rubric language in all assessment feedback:
- "Sets up but does not complete"
- "Correct process with computation error"
- "Justification incomplete"
- "Correct interpretation"

## Differentiation

### For Struggling Students

- **Before class:** Preview material, pre-teach vocabulary
- **During class:** Strategic pairing, additional scaffolding
- **After class:** Office hours, remediation resources
- **Visualizations:** Start with concrete, build to abstract

### For Advanced Students

- **Extension problems:** More challenging applications
- **Proof exercises:** Rigorous justification tasks
- **Historical context:** How concepts developed
- **Connections:** Links to multivariable calculus, analysis

### For Zoom Students

- **Visual clarity:** All text readable at small scale
- **Audio quality:** Clear verbal explanations
- **Interaction:** Polls, chat participation, breakout rooms
- **Recording:** Sessions recorded for review

## Presentation Design Standards

All presentations adhere to `master-presentation-spec.md`. Key principles:

### Readability
- Minimum 1.5rem font for primary content
- High contrast (4.5:1 ratio minimum)
- Dark mode reduces eye strain

### Structure
- One concept per slide
- Maximum 6 items in any list
- Consistent navigation (keyboard shortcuts)
- Clear learning objective on every presentation

### Visualizations
- All D3 text at 1.25rem minimum
- Proportional sizing (rem, not px)
- Consistent color coding across course
- Animation serves understanding

## Course Outcomes

By the end of this course, students will:

### Understand
- Why the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus works (not just how to apply it)
- How derivatives and integrals are inverses of each other
- Why series can converge to finite values
- How calculus connects to real-world phenomena

### Be Able To
- Apply calculus procedures accurately
- Translate between graphical, numerical, analytical, and verbal representations
- Justify answers with mathematical reasoning
- Communicate mathematical ideas precisely

### Feel
- Confident approaching novel problems
- Curious about mathematical patterns
- Prepared for the AP exam
- Capable of further mathematics study
