Extreme Value Theorem & Critical Points

Finding Maximum and Minimum Values

Class 25 | CED Topic 5.2 | AP Calculus BC | NCSSM

Where does a function reach its highest and lowest values?

And what do we know about the derivative there?

Warm-Up

Consider \( f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 3 \) on the interval \( [0, 5] \).

  1. Sketch the parabola
  2. Find where \( f'(x) = 0 \)
  3. What are the highest and lowest values of \( f \) on \( [0, 5] \)?

Absolute (Global) Extrema

Absolute Maximum and Minimum

Let \( f \) be defined on a domain \( D \) containing \( c \).

\( f(c) \) is an absolute maximum if \( f(c) \geq f(x) \) for all \( x \) in \( D \).

\( f(c) \) is an absolute minimum if \( f(c) \leq f(x) \) for all \( x \) in \( D \).

These are the overall highest and lowest points on the entire domain.

Local (Relative) Extrema

Local Maximum and Minimum

\( f(c) \) is a local maximum if \( f(c) \geq f(x) \) for all \( x \) near \( c \).

\( f(c) \) is a local minimum if \( f(c) \leq f(x) \) for all \( x \) near \( c \).

Highest or lowest in a neighborhood, but not necessarily on the whole domain.

The Extreme Value Theorem

Extreme Value Theorem (EVT)

If \( f \) is continuous on a closed interval \( [a, b] \),

then \( f \) attains an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on \( [a, b] \).

Key insight: The theorem guarantees existence, but doesn't tell us where the extrema occur!

EVT in Action

On a closed interval, continuous functions must attain their extrema

When EVT Doesn't Apply

Not Closed

\( f(x) = x \) on \( (0, 1) \)

No max or min!

Not Continuous

\( f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \) on \( [-1, 1] \)

Discontinuous at 0

Not Bounded

\( f(x) = x^2 \) on \( \mathbb{R} \)

Unbounded domain

Critical Points

Definition: Critical Point

A critical point of \( f \) is a value \( c \) in the domain of \( f \) where:

\[ f'(c) = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad f'(c) \text{ does not exist} \]

Critical points are the only candidates for local extrema!

Types of Critical Points

\( f'(c) = 0 \) (horizontal tangent)
\( f'(c) \) DNE (corner/cusp)

Fermat's Theorem

Fermat's Theorem

If \( f \) has a local maximum or minimum at \( c \), and \( f'(c) \) exists, then:

\[ f'(c) = 0 \]

Caution!

The converse is false! \( f'(c) = 0 \) does not guarantee a local extremum.

Example: \( f(x) = x^3 \) at \( x = 0 \)

Example: Finding Critical Points

Find all critical points of \( f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 - 9x + 5 \).

1 \( f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x - 9 = 3(x^2 - 2x - 3) \)
2 Factor: \( f'(x) = 3(x - 3)(x + 1) \)
3 Set \( f'(x) = 0 \): \( x = 3 \) or \( x = -1 \)
4 Critical points: \( x = -1 \) and \( x = 3 \)

Example: Derivative Does Not Exist

Find all critical points of \( f(x) = |x - 2| + 1 \).

1 Rewrite: \( f(x) = \begin{cases} -(x-2) + 1 & x < 2 \\ (x-2) + 1 & x \geq 2 \end{cases} \)
2 \( f'(x) = \begin{cases} -1 & x < 2 \\ 1 & x > 2 \end{cases} \)
3 \( f'(2) \) does not exist (corner)
4 Critical point: \( x = 2 \) (and it's a local minimum!)

The Closed Interval Method

Finding Absolute Extrema on \( [a, b] \)

  1. Find all critical points of \( f \) in \( (a, b) \)
  2. Evaluate \( f \) at each critical point
  3. Evaluate \( f \) at the endpoints \( a \) and \( b \)
  4. Compare all values: largest is abs max, smallest is abs min

Example: Closed Interval Method

Find the absolute extrema of \( f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1 \) on \( [-2, 2] \).

1 \( f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1 \)
2 Evaluate: \( f(-2) = -1 \), \( f(-1) = 3 \), \( f(1) = -1 \), \( f(2) = 3 \)
3 Abs max = 3 at \( x = -1 \) and \( x = 2 \)
4 Abs min = -1 at \( x = -2 \) and \( x = 1 \)

Exploring Extrema: \( f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1 \)

f(-2) = -1
f(-1) = 3
f(1) = -1
f(2) = 3

Key Takeaways

EVT: Continuous on closed interval = extrema exist

Critical Points: Where \( f'(c) = 0 \) or \( f'(c) \) DNE

Closed Interval Method: Compare critical points and endpoints

Local extrema occur only at critical points, but not every critical point is an extremum!
