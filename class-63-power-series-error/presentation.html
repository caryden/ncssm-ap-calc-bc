Power Series and Error Bounds

Class 63 | CED Topics 10.10, 10.12, 10.13

Convergence and Approximation Error

Warm-Up

The Maclaurin series for \( e^x \) is \( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} \)

If we use \( P_4(x) \) to approximate \( e^{0.5} \), how do we know how accurate it is?

Radius of Convergence

A power series \( \sum c_n(x-a)^n \) converges for \( |x-a| < R \)

\( R \) = radius of convergence

• \( R = 0 \): converges only at \( x = a \)

• \( R = \infty \): converges for all \( x \)

• \( 0 < R < \infty \): converges on interval

Finding Radius of Convergence

Use the Ratio Test on the power series:

\( L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{c_{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1}}{c_n(x-a)^n} \right| = |x-a| \cdot \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{c_{n+1}}{c_n} \right| \)

Converges when \( L < 1 \), so:

\( R = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{c_n}{c_{n+1}} \right| \)

Example: Find Radius

Find the radius of convergence for \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n} \)

\( L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} \cdot \frac{n}{x^n} \right| = |x| \cdot \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{n+1} = |x| \)

Converges when \( |x| < 1 \), so \( R = 1 \)

Check endpoints: \( x = 1 \) gives harmonic (diverges), \( x = -1 \) gives alternating harmonic (converges)

Lagrange Error Bound

For Taylor polynomial \( P_n(x) \) centered at \( a \):

\( |R_n(x)| = |f(x) - P_n(x)| \leq \frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1} \)

where \( M = \max |f^{(n+1)}(z)| \) for \( z \) between \( a \) and \( x \)

Understanding the Error Bound

What You Need

1. The value of \( n \)
2. The center \( a \)
3. The value \( x \)
4. Bound on \( |f^{(n+1)}| \)

What It Tells You

The maximum possible error when using \( P_n(x) \) to approximate \( f(x) \)

Example: Error Bound for \( e^x \)

Bound the error when using \( P_3(x) \) to approximate \( e^{0.5} \).

For \( e^x \): \( f^{(4)}(x) = e^x \)

For \( 0 \leq z \leq 0.5 \): \( |f^{(4)}(z)| \leq e^{0.5} < e < 3 \)

\( |R_3(0.5)| \leq \frac{3}{4!}(0.5)^4 = \frac{3}{24} \cdot \frac{1}{16} = \frac{3}{384} < 0.008 \)

Alternating Series Error Bound

For a convergent alternating series \( \sum (-1)^n a_n \):

\( |S - S_n| \leq a_{n+1} \)

The error is at most the first omitted term!
(Often easier than Lagrange for alternating series)

Choosing an Error Bound

Lagrange Error

Use when:
• Series is not alternating
• Need specific bound
• All terms same sign

Alternating Series

Use when:
• Series alternates
• Terms decrease
• Often simpler!

Example: Error for \( \sin(0.1) \)

Using \( P_5(x) = x - \frac{x^3}{6} + \frac{x^5}{120} \)

Method 1 (Alternating): Next term is \( -\frac{(0.1)^7}{7!} \)

Error \( \leq \frac{10^{-7}}{5040} \approx 2 \times 10^{-11} \)

Incredibly small error — polynomials are powerful!

How Many Terms Do I Need?

To approximate \( \cos(1) \) with error < 0.001:

Series: \( 1 - \frac{1}{2!} + \frac{1}{4!} - \frac{1}{6!} + \ldots \) (alternating)

Need \( \frac{1}{(n+1)!} < 0.001 \)

\( \frac{1}{6!} = \frac{1}{720} \approx 0.0014 > 0.001 \)
\( \frac{1}{8!} = \frac{1}{40320} \approx 0.000025 < 0.001 \) ✓

Use terms through \( \frac{x^6}{6!} \) (i.e., \( P_6(x) \))

Interval of Convergence

After finding radius \( R \), check endpoints separately:

At \( x = a - R \): Substitute and test convergence

At \( x = a + R \): Substitute and test convergence

Possible intervals: \( (a-R, a+R) \), \( [a-R, a+R) \), \( (a-R, a+R] \), \( [a-R, a+R] \)

Key Takeaways

• Radius of convergence: use Ratio Test on power series

• Lagrange Error: \( |R_n| \leq \frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1} \)

• Alternating series error: \( \leq \) first omitted term

• Check endpoints separately for interval of convergence

• Error bounds let us know how accurate our approximations are
